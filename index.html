Walking in a Good Way

A Three-Part Course for Non-Native Visitors to Sacred Land
Prepared for Quaker Communities · Grounded in Indigenous Teachings
In partnership with the Great Plains Action Society Urban Resilience Hub, Iowa City ↗

"Land is the only thing that is real."

— Sikowis Nobiss, Cree/Saulteaux, founder of the Great Plains Action Society

This course prepares non-Native people — particularly those in Quaker communities — to visit sacred and community land in a good way. It covers history we must know, protocols we must practice, and relationships we must sustain.
A note on land acknowledgment: Iowa City sits on the unceded territory of the Meskwaki (Sac and Fox), Baxoje (Iowa Nation), and Yankton Sioux peoples — among others. Acknowledging this is a beginning, not an end. This course asks us to go much further.

📋 How to Use This Course

This is a three-session course designed for adult education in Quaker meetings, youth groups, or any community of faith preparing for a visit to Indigenous community land. Each session stands alone but gains depth when taken in sequence.

The sessions follow a natural arc: Before you go · While you're there · After you return. Each session includes history, teachings, reflection queries, and a connection to the work happening at the Great Plains Action Society Urban Resilience Hub in Iowa City, Iowa.

Format: Each session is designed for 60–90 minutes, though they can be expanded for retreats. This digital guide works well projected during group sessions, or explored individually beforehand.

🌾 The Great Plains Action Society Urban Resilience Hub

In the southside of Iowa City, at 810 Maiden Lane, the Great Plains Action Society (GPAS) is building something rare: an urban, Indigenous-led community hub on land they own.

"We're building a micro-community. Like a Petri dish growing until we fill the entire area… until we fill the entire world." — Sikowis Nobiss, Little Village, March 2026

The hub will include a healing sanctuary, teaching kitchen, gathering area, classrooms, mutual aid garden, and an earthlodge. The Spring Equinox 2026 ceremony — marked by the Buffalo Eagle Singers, Ioway Dancers, First Foods, and a land blessing — opened this space in a good way. It is open to all, with a particular focus on people historically pushed to the margins: Indigenous communities, people of color, women, LGBTQ and Two-Spirit people, disabled people, and migrants.

🕊 Why Quakers? Why Now?

Quakers hold testimonies of Integrity, Equality, and Community. But our history with Native peoples demands we look honestly at what we have done — not just what we have believed. Quakers ran more than 30 Indian boarding schools. We strongly supported President Grant's 1869 "Peace Policy," which aimed to erase Indigenous cultures by separating children from their families.

At the same time, contemporary Quakers are among those working hardest to repair these harms — through the "Toward Right Relationship with Native Peoples" program, through formal apologies, and through organizations like the Decolonial Repair Network.

This course sits in that tension: honest about past harm, committed to present repair.

📜 The Mirror We Must Look Into: Quaker History with Native Peoples

Tap any item below to learn more. Honesty about this history is the first step in visiting sacred land with integrity.

1796
First Quaker schools for Native children established
Baltimore Friends established one of the first Quaker missions among the Oneida Nation. From the beginning, "civilizing" Native peoples was inseparable from erasing their culture. Quaker intentions were often described as benevolent — but the harm was real.
1819
Indian Civilization Act — Quakers receive federal funding
Congress passed the Indian Civilization Act, authorizing the federal government to fund religious organizations to "civilize" Native peoples. Quakers were prominent recipients, running day schools and boarding schools on Indigenous lands.
1869
Grant's "Peace Policy" — Quakers given oversight of Native agencies
Quakers were strong supporters of President Grant's "Peace Policy," which placed Christian denominations in charge of Native American reservations to accelerate assimilation. This policy gave rise to the boarding school system under the philosophy "Kill the Indian, Save the Man."
1870s–1960s
The boarding school era: over 500 schools, thousands of children
The federal government, with the active support of Quakers and other Christian bodies, operated a system of boarding schools designed to permanently separate Indigenous children from their families, tribal nations, faith, culture, and identity. Quakers ran at least 30 of these schools. Children faced forced labor, physical punishment, and the prohibition of their languages and ceremonies.
2018
First Nation–Farmer Climate Unity March, Iowa
A small group of Native and non-Native people walked 94 miles along the path of the Dakota Access Pipeline, from Des Moines to Fort Dodge, Iowa — Sept. 1–8, 2018. The eight-day walk was about something radical: listening. "The purpose was to spend the 8 days sharing stories with each other so we could work on things of common concern. That worked extremely well." — Jeff Kisling
2022
Alaska Quakers formally apologize to Alaska Native communities
In a landmark act, Alaska Quakers formally apologized to Alaska Native communities for the residential schools they operated — schools that forcibly assimilated and abused Indigenous children, separated them from their families, and caused intergenerational trauma. This was one of the first formal Quaker apologies of its kind.
2023
Honor Native Land Fund launches in Iowa
The Decolonial Repair Network launched the Honor Native Land Fund, initially focused on supporting the Great Plains Action Society's Urban Resilience Hub in Iowa City. The fund represents a shift "beyond land acknowledgments" to practical acts of repair — rematriating land to Indigenous stewardship.
2026
Spring Equinox opens the Resilience Hub in Iowa City
On March 21, 2026, a land blessing ceremony at 810 Maiden Lane, Iowa City — with the Buffalo Eagle Singers, Ioway Dancers, prayers, and a First Foods meal — opened the Great Plains Action Society's new community hub. "It wasn't necessarily a grand opening," said Sikowis Nobiss. "It was the beginning of us in our space starting in a good way."

🗺 Whose Land Are We Standing On?

Iowa City and the surrounding region sit on lands with deep Indigenous history. Before visiting any site, know this land belongs to living Nations, not just history:

  • Meskwaki (Sac and Fox): Still present in Iowa today. The Meskwaki Settlement near Tama is the only land in Iowa owned by Indigenous people.
  • Baxoje (Iowa Nation): Original inhabitants of Iowa. Removed by treaty to Kansas and Oklahoma, though connections to the land remain.
  • Yankton Sioux: Among the many Sioux nations with historical presence in Iowa territory.
"We build power through community. And community is what was taken from us." — Sikowis Nobiss, Little Village, March 2026

🧭 Jeff's Journey: How It Started

Jeff's Story · Session One

In September 2018, Jeff Kisling walked 94 miles alongside Native farmers, activists, and allies along the route of the Dakota Access Pipeline. He didn't go as an expert. He went to listen. "The purpose was to spend the 8 days sharing stories with each other so we could work on things of common concern." The march introduced him to relationships that would lead him — years later — to the planning committee of the Decolonial Repair Network and to volunteer work at the Resilience Hub in Iowa City.

The first step was not a grand gesture. It was showing up to walk.

    Ask permission and confirm the invitation. Do not visit without being genuinely welcomed.

    Research that community's specific protocols — they vary between Nations. Ask if you're unsure.
  • ✓
    Prepare a gift to bring. Tobacco, food, or a monetary contribution are commonly appropriate. Ask in advance what is welcome.
  • ✓
    Set a clear intention: "I come to listen and to learn, not to teach or evaluate."
  • ✓
    Talk with your group about the history of Quakers and Native peoples. Do this before you go — not during.
  • ✓
    Leave your schedule loose. Sacred time is not clock time. Plan to wait, and plan to stay longer than expected.
  • ✓
    Brief younger participants or those new to this: what they may see, hear, or be invited to do.
  • ✓
    Ask yourself honestly: What do I hope to get from this? Check that this is not a transaction.

🌀 Queries for Reflection · Session One

Use these queries for silent reflection, journaling, or group discussion in the manner of Friends.

  • What is my family's relationship to the land we live on now? Do I know who lived here before us?
  • What do I hope to receive from this visit? What am I prepared to give — in time, presence, and resources?
  • How do I hold the complexity that Quakers both caused great harm to Native peoples and are among those working hardest for repair today?
  • The Quaker testimony of Integrity calls us to be honest even when the truth is uncomfortable. What truth about my own history have I been avoiding?
  • What is the difference between "helping" Indigenous communities and walking alongside them as a peer?

Session Two: Being in the Place

How to be present on sacred land. Listening, participating, and respecting what you encounter.

"You can't just sit down and hit the drum. You gotta be respectful of that drum." — Ukeegray (Nate Scates), Buffalo Eagle Singers, GPAS Spring Equinox, 2026

🚪 The Entryway Teaching

Many Indigenous traditions practice the "entryway ceremony" when visiting another Nation's territory. You announce yourself. You come singing — not surprising people, but allowing them to know you are arriving in peace. You cleanse yourself of hardship or negative energy before crossing the threshold, so you do not bring it into that space. You come with gifts, and you come asking permission.

As a non-Native visitor, you are unlikely to know the specific songs or ceremonies. But the spirit of the entryway teaching applies: arrive with intention and humility, not as a consumer arriving for a service.

At the Resilience Hub in Iowa City, Sikowis Nobiss opened the Spring Equinox by saying: "It was the beginning of us in our space starting in a good way." That phrase — starting in a good way — is the heart of this session.

👂 Listening as Sacred Practice

Quaker worship is built on the practice of gathered waiting — holding silence until something rises that needs to be spoken. This is, perhaps, the best preparation for visiting Indigenous community spaces. The skill is the same: resist the urge to fill silence. Sit with discomfort. Trust that what comes from the other person has value you cannot manufacture yourself.

"There's no competition in the circle. That dance is for everyone." — Christina Wedner, dancer at GPAS Spring Equinox, 2026

The circle — in ceremony, in talking circles, in round dances — is a teaching about equality and relation. No one is above or below. You are a participant in something larger than your own story.

Jeff's Story · Session Two
During work sessions at the Resilience Hub, Jeff has learned that showing up to work — to pull weeds, move materials, share a meal — is its own form of listening. The relationship is built not in presentations or ceremonies alone, but in the ordinary work of being present over time.

🃏 Protocol Cards · Tap to flip each card

These protocols are rooted in authentic Indigenous teachings and in the specific context of the GPAS Resilience Hub. Protocols vary between Nations — always ask when in doubt.

📸
Photography
tap to flip
Ask permission before every photo. Never photograph ceremony, sacred items, or individuals without explicit consent. When in doubt, put the camera away.
Why: images can be shared and used without consent, stripping sacred practices of their context and power.
🪶
Sacred Items
tap to flip
Do not touch sacred items — drums, feathers, medicine bundles — unless explicitly invited to do so. Observe, don't handle.
Why: sacred items carry spiritual responsibility. Handling them without invitation can cause harm to both the item and the handler.
🌿
Smudging
tap to flip
Participate if you feel genuinely led. It is also acceptable to respectfully decline. Do not perform participation to appear open-minded.
Why: smudging is a spiritual cleansing practice. Authentic participation means entering it sincerely, not for show.
🥁
The Drum
tap to flip
Observe and receive drum singing with reverence. Do not touch a drum without invitation. Respond inwardly; you are in the presence of prayer.
"You can't just sit down and hit the drum. You gotta be respectful of that drum." — Nate Scates
🍲
Food & Meals
tap to flip
Accept food that is offered, with gratitude. First Foods (pre-colonial, traditional foods) are ceremonial as well as nourishing. Receive them as a gift.
Sharing food is an act of kinship and trust. Refusing without a genuine allergy can be a slight.
📵
Phone & Devices
tap to flip
Put your phone away during ceremony and teachings. No texting, no calls, no social media. Your full attention is the most basic form of respect.
Divided attention signals that you believe something elsewhere matters more than what is happening here.
🤫
Silence
tap to flip
Be comfortable in silence. Indigenous ceremony often involves long pauses that carry meaning. Do not try to fill them. Let them work on you.
As Quakers, we know that the most important things can arrive in silence. This is a place where that practice is essential.
🌀
Asking Questions
tap to flip
Ask, don't assume. If you don't understand something, ask respectfully, privately, and at an appropriate moment. Never ask during ceremony.
Assumptions and immediate questions can interrupt the flow of what is happening. Let the experience unfold, then ask.

🏡 At the Great Plains Action Society Resilience Hub, Iowa City

810 Maiden Lane is being built as a healing space — land that has been harmed is being actively restored. When you enter, you are entering a living act of repair.

  • Arrive a few minutes early and orient yourself quietly before others gather.
  • Introduce yourself to your host and ask if there are specific protocols for that day.
  • Follow the lead of community members — when to sit, when to stand, when to join a circle.
  • If there is work to be done, offer to help with the physical tasks — gardening, building, cooking — not just the "learning events."
  • The Hub centers Indigenous people, people of color, women, and those historically marginalized. Non-Native visitors are guests, not the audience.
"This land has been harmed. And we're going to continue to heal the land, and with it, heal ourselves." — Sikowis Nobiss

🚶 What the March Taught Us About Lasting Relationship

In September 2018, a small group of Native and non-Native people walked 94 miles through Iowa along the route of the Dakota Access Pipeline — from Des Moines to Fort Dodge. The walk took eight days. The purpose wasn't protest alone. It was relationship.

Jeff's Story · Session Three
"The purpose was to spend the 8 days sharing stories with each other, so we could work on things of common concern. That worked extremely well and various combinations of us have worked on numerous things since." — Jeff Kisling

The march was not the destination. The connections made during those eight days became the foundation for years of collaborative work: Des Moines Mutual Aid, the Decolonial Repair Network, the Resilience Hub. The walk was a beginning.
"This gathering helped to give a sense to everyone that this is about real relationships." — Eric Anglada, DRN co-founder, 2025

🌉 From Visitor to Ally: What's the Difference?

A visitor takes an experience and returns home to their regular life. An ally returns, shows up for work sessions, gives money, makes phone calls, brings others into relationship, and accepts that this work is lifelong.

"Decolonization is a way of trying to undo, repair and heal the primary injustice on this continent. All of us — Native and non-Native — are haunted in a way and damaged in a way over these five-plus centuries." — Eric Anglada, Decolonial Repair Network, 2025

The Decolonial Repair Network hosts monthly calls where non-Native allies discuss practical ways to support land return and Indigenous-led initiatives. Members are encouraged to make monthly financial contributions, grow the network, and examine their own family's colonial history.

"This isn't an organization but an organism, and we want to be growing and taking shape as the work unfolds." — Eric Anglada

🕊 Share This Course

The most immediate act of carrying it forward: teach what you have learned to other non-Native people in your meeting, school, or community. You don't need to be an expert. You need to have walked this path first.

🕊 Bringing the Learning to Your Meeting

The Quaker business process offers a powerful tool: the minute. A meeting can adopt a minute of commitment — to research its involvement in boarding schools, to financially support Indigenous-led land return, or to begin regular education on this history. New England Yearly Meeting adopted such a minute in 2022.

But the minute comes after the learning. This course is the learning. Suggest to your meeting's adult education committee that this course become a regular offering — especially before any group visit to the Resilience Hub or other sacred land.

"We wanted to move beyond land acknowledgments and teach the real history. It's also important to engage in practical acts of repair." — Eric Anglada, DRN co-founder

🌀 Queries for the Long Work · Session Three

  • What is one concrete, time-bound commitment I can make to continue this relationship — not as a visitor, but as an ally?
  • How will I share what I have learned with people who were not present? What are my responsibilities to pass this on?
  • Where is the Spirit calling me in this work? Is that different from where it is convenient to be called?
  • The First Nation–Farmer Climate Unity March took eight days to change relationships that lasted years. What is my equivalent — what am I willing to give time to?
  • If "land is the only thing that is real," what does my relationship to the land I live on say about how I am living?
